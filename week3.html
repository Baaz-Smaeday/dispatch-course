üîä Listen:

In the UK, any business operating HGVs (Heavy Goods Vehicles) over 3.5 tonnes on public roads must hold an Operator's Licence (O-Licence). This is issued by the Traffic Commissioner and regulated by the DVSA (Driver and Vehicle Standards Agency) ‚Äî the UK equivalent of the USA's FMCSA.

üîµ Three Types of O-Licence:

Restricted Licence: Only for own goods ‚Äî cannot carry goods for hire. Example: A supermarket carrying its own stock.

Standard National: Carry goods for hire within the UK only. Most common for UK hauliers.

Standard International: Carry goods for hire within UK AND internationally (EU, post-Brexit requires extra documentation). Required for any cross-border work.
üìã O-Licence Requirements ‚Äî What a Carrier Must Have:
‚Ä¢ Established business in the UK with a physical operating centre
‚Ä¢ Good repute ‚Äî no serious criminal convictions, no transport law violations
‚Ä¢ Financial standing ‚Äî minimum ¬£8,000 for first vehicle + ¬£4,500 per additional vehicle
‚Ä¢ Professional competence ‚Äî Transport Manager must hold CPC (Certificate of Professional Competence)
‚Ä¢ Operating centre ‚Äî a secure place to park vehicles when not in use
üéØ As a Dispatcher ‚Äî What You Must Check:
Before working with any UK carrier, verify their O-Licence is valid and active on the DVSA public register at vehicle-operator-licensing.service.gov.uk
Check: Licence number | Type (Standard National or International) | Authorised vehicles | Expiry date
A carrier without a valid O-Licence operating commercially is committing a criminal offence.
‚ö†Ô∏è DVSA Enforcement: DVSA enforcement officers can stop vehicles on the road at any time. If a vehicle is found without a valid O-Licence, the vehicle can be impounded, the driver prosecuted, and the carrier's licence revoked. As a dispatcher, never book a load for a carrier you cannot verify.

UK Drivers' Hours rules are strictly enforced by DVSA roadside checks and tachograph analysis. As a dispatcher, you must plan loads within legal driving windows ‚Äî violating these rules can result in prosecution of both the driver AND the operator.

üïê Daily Driving Limits:
‚Ä¢ Maximum 9 hours driving per day
‚Ä¢ Can be extended to 10 hours ‚Äî but only twice per calendar week
‚Ä¢ After 4.5 hours continuous driving ‚Äî mandatory 45-minute break (can be split 15 min + 30 min in that order)
‚Ä¢ Minimum daily rest: 11 hours (can be reduced to 9 hours ‚Äî maximum 3 times per week)
üìÖ Weekly & Fortnightly Limits:
‚Ä¢ Weekly driving limit: 56 hours maximum
‚Ä¢ Fortnightly (2-week) limit: 90 hours maximum
‚Ä¢ Weekly rest: 45 consecutive hours minimum
‚Ä¢ Reduced weekly rest (24 hours) allowed ‚Äî but must be compensated within 3 weeks
‚è± Tachograph Modes ‚Äî What the Driver Must Select:
üöó D ‚Äî Driving: Vehicle in motion ‚Äî recorded automatically
üîß H ‚Äî Other Work: Loading, paperwork, waiting at a job (not rest)
üìç A ‚Äî Availability: Waiting but not responsible for vehicle (e.g. waiting at ferry)
üò¥ X ‚Äî Rest: Off duty, sleeping, genuine rest period

Tachograph data is stored for 365 days. DVSA can request it at any time.
‚ö†Ô∏è Dispatcher's Legal Responsibility:
Under UK law, a dispatcher (or transport manager) who instructs a driver to exceed hours limits can be prosecuted. Common scenarios:
‚Ä¢ Booking a load requiring the driver to drive 11 hours when they only have 9 hours available
‚Ä¢ Not checking tachograph hours before assigning next load
‚Ä¢ Pressuring driver to skip mandatory 45-minute break
Always ask: "How many hours do you have available today?" before booking any load.
üéØ Practical Planning Example:
Driver starts at 6:00 AM. Drive 4.5 hrs ‚Üí mandatory break 45 min ‚Üí drive up to 4.5 hrs more.
Earliest they can leave delivery = 6:00 AM + 4.5 + 0.75 + 4.5 = 3:45 PM (9.75 hrs elapsed, 9 hrs driving).
Daily rest must start by 9:00 PM (15 hr window from start). Plan loads within this window.

üá¨üáß UK Domestic Loads ‚Äî Consignment Note:
For loads moving entirely within the UK, carriers use a Consignment Note (also called a delivery note or dispatch note). This is much simpler than a CMR ‚Äî it typically shows: sender, receiver, description of goods, weight, reference number, driver signature at pickup and receiver signature at delivery.

Some larger shippers use their own branded paperwork ‚Äî the content is similar. The signed delivery note = your proof of delivery = triggers your invoice.
üåç International Loads (UK‚ÄìEU) ‚Äî CMR Note:
The CMR Note (Convention on the Contract for the International Carriage of Goods by Road) is the legally required document for all international road freight.

Must be issued in at least 3 originals: Sender keeps one | One travels with the goods | Carrier keeps one.

CMR liability cap: 8.33 SDR per kg of lost/damaged goods (approx ¬£9‚Äì10/kg at current rates).

Post-Brexit requirement: UK‚ÄìEU loads now also need Safety & Security declaration (ENS/EXS), EORI number, customs commodity codes, and may need an import/export licence for certain goods.

Domestic load: ‚úÖ Consignment note | ‚úÖ Vehicle O-Licence | ‚úÖ Driver CPC card | ‚úÖ Tachograph card

Courier Exchange (CX) is the UK's largest freight exchange with over 6,000 member companies. It is the UK equivalent of DAT in the USA ‚Äî but works differently because UK freight is priced per load or per pallet, not per mile.

üñ•Ô∏è What Courier Exchange Offers:
‚Ä¢ Real-time load and vehicle postings across UK and Europe
‚Ä¢ Member credit ratings ‚Äî check reliability before accepting a load
‚Ä¢ In-app messaging and document sharing
‚Ä¢ Track and trace integration
‚Ä¢ Available work alerts by postcode area
‚Ä¢ CX mobile app for drivers
Cost: Approximately ¬£120‚Äì200/month membership depending on vehicle type
üí∑ How UK Freight Rates Work (Very Different from USA):
UK rates are typically quoted as:
‚Ä¢ Per pallet: Standard UK pallet (120cm √ó 100cm √ó 120cm high) ‚Äî typical rate ¬£20‚Äì80 per pallet depending on lane and urgency
‚Ä¢ Per load (FTL): Full trailer/truck rate ‚Äî typical 13.6m curtainsider: ¬£600‚Äì2,500 depending on distance and lane
‚Ä¢ Per mile/km: Used for courier and specialist work ‚Äî typically ¬£1.50‚Äì3.50/mile for vans, ¬£2.50‚Äì5.00/mile for HGV

Always convert to cost-per-mile to compare loads properly.
üîÑ Other UK Load Boards to Know:
Haulage Exchange (TEG): Part of Transport Exchange Group ‚Äî good for owner-operators. Lower cost than CX.
Palletways, Palletforce, Pall-Ex, TPN: These are UK pallet NETWORKS ‚Äî not load boards. Subcontractor pallet work (covered in Day 13).
Direct Freight: Smaller UK load board, useful for same-day courier work.
Shiply: B2C and small business loads ‚Äî not for professional HGV work.
Email/WhatsApp groups: Many UK owner-operators still find work through direct relationships and WhatsApp groups ‚Äî build these over time.

UK pallet networks are a unique and stable income source for small carriers. They provide a steady, predictable flow of pallet collections and deliveries in a defined geographic area ‚Äî known as a depot franchise territory.

üîÑ How Pallet Networks Work:
1. Shippers book pallets through a network member
2. Local member collects pallets and trunks them overnight to the central hub
3. Hub sorts and sends pallets out to the destination depot
4. Destination depot delivers next morning

This means your carrier collects in their local area, trunks overnight, and delivers locally at destination ‚Äî highly efficient routing with minimal deadhead.
üì¶ UK's Big 4 Pallet Networks:

Palletforce: Owned by Eddie Stobart Group. ~100 depot members. Strong in Midlands and North England.

Pall-Ex: One of UK's oldest pallet networks. ~90 depots. Strong European reach via Pall-Ex Europe.

The Pallet Network (TPN): ~100+ UK members. Competitive on pricing. Strong in South and West England.

Palletways: Part of Imperial group. European network ‚Äî good for cross-border pallet work. Strongest in the South East.

Hazchem Network: Specialist ‚Äî hazardous goods pallets. Requires ADR certification.
